Interviews : Question Techniques

  • Recommended reading:
    • Cracking the Coding Interview
    • Programming Challenges by Skiena
  • Solve problems both on a PC, and on a pen+paper
  • Finish problems to the end, regardless of how bad your answer may be.
  • Recommended solving method:
    • Begin with a brute force solution.
    • Note it down.
    • Simplify and optimize parts of the solution.
      • Incremental, structured optimization is effective.
    • Interviewers will have a positive attitude towards this method, as they can witness your thought process.
  • Always remember to solve problems in pen/pencil, and write/speak your way through what the solution is doing.
  • Generate reasoning for applying certain algorithms to certain problems.
  • Time yourself explaining your answers.

Strategic, Effective Learning

  • Don't be afraid of difficulty. Capability comes automatically with the will to learn.
  • Learn X by doing X.
    • Find a good book, class, or other means of learning, prevent yourself from 'skimming'. Finish the most easy-seeming examples.
      • Create related exercises after you have finished one.
      • Break things on purpose, so you can see when and how errors are generated.
  • Find an idea of a project to build, and apply your learning to it whenever possible.
  • Attempt to commit to your project at least a little bit every day, where possible; keeping it fresh in your mind, and ensuring memory.
  • Remember that you're learning a lot of things, and some of the things you're learning will make other things easier - an incentive for memorising and practicing all topics.
  • Don't be afraid to start small.
  • Don't be afraid to grow big.
  • Don't be afraid of having to restart.

Programming Practices

  • When a variable will be used in more than just a small area (e.g. a for loop), single-letter names are bad.
  • You don't always need a class:
    • Are you combining data and routines?
    • Will your class be used more than once?
    • Would another developer assume that you would have created a class?
  • You don't always need to write a routine:
    • Will the routine be used more than once or twice?
    • Is it accessible by all parts of the program that need it?
  • Documentation is part of your software. Don't forget it.
  • Write comments, but only when they're necessary.
    • If your code is difficult to understand:
      • You might have written it badly.
      • You might have named variables undescriptively.
      • If both of those things are false, then commenting is needed.
  • Don't ignore a language's standard library. There could be a plethora of things to make your life easier that you are not noticing.
  • The compiler doesn't mind if you split your code up. Organise it neatly, don't have an enormous main function, and don't have spaghetti code.
  • You will never 'know' all of a programming language.
